### The Problem: Instructions Frame Narrative Mechanics, Not Recognition

The current prompt describes sections in terms of what the LLM should *do* (narrative mechanics) rather than what the reader should *feel* (recognition objectives). This is why Opus succeeds and Haiku fails with the same structure â€” Opus naturally writes for recognition, Haiku follows structural instructions literally and produces evidence without soul.

### Proposed Instruction Revisions

**Opening â€” current:** "Start with BREADTH â€” an impressionistic read of the whole person"
**Opening â€” revised:** "The reader must feel HEARD within the first 3 sentences. Use specific things they said â€” not summaries, not impressions. Then the spine drop should name a tension they recognize but haven't articulated."

1. **Build: cap at 2 sub-sections** â€” self-recognition has diminishing returns after the 2nd observation
2. **Turn: maximum 2 paragraphs after the crystallization sentence** â€” silence after the drop gives it weight

## What If Scenarios

### CRITICAL: Spine Selection (Highest-Priority Vulnerability)

The entire portrait architecture hangs on ONE organizing element. If the spine is wrong, nothing downstream recovers. This is the single point of failure â€” observed across all models, even Opus, and significantly worse on Sonnet and Haiku.

**Why models pick the wrong spine:**

| What models gravitate to | Why it's wrong |
|---|---|
| The most **dramatic** event | Dramatic â‰  central. Career dissatisfaction is dramatic but it's a symptom, not the pattern |
| The most **discussed** topic | Repetition â‰  importance. The reader talked about it a lot because it's on their mind, not because it defines them |
| The **easiest to write about** | A clear contradiction is easier to narrate. The model optimizes for its own fluency, not the reader's recognition |
| A **surface-level observation** | "You keep abandoning plans" is surface. "You navigate by a signal you haven't named" is underneath |

**The core distinction the prompt must enforce:**

- "You keep abandoning plans" â†’ something that HAPPENED (surface) â€” not a spine
- "You navigate by pull, not by map" â†’ something that IS TRUE ABOUT THEM (underneath) â€” this is a spine
- The spine is NEVER an event. It's the pattern underneath the events.

**Proposed spine selection process:**

```
BEFORE YOU WRITE â€” FIND YOUR THREAD (revised)

Step 1: Identify 2-3 candidate spines from the evidence.

Step 2: For each candidate, test:
  - How many facets does it connect? (More = stronger)
  - Is it surface or underneath? (Underneath = stronger)
  - The "across the table" test: imagine saying it to this
    person face to face. Would they go quiet with recognition?
    â†’ That's a spine. Would they say "yeah, I know"?
    â†’ That's a fact, go deeper.

Step 3: Choose the one that goes deepest AND connects the most.
A spine that explains WHY five things happen beats one that
describes WHAT one dramatic thing is.
```

**Anti-patterns for spine selection:**

```
Bad spines (common model mistakes):
- The most dramatic event in the conversation (that's evidence,
  not the spine)
- The topic discussed most often (repetition â‰  centrality)
- A trait label rephrased ("you're someone who values authenticity"
  â€” generic, could be anyone)
- The thing easiest to write about (optimizing for YOUR fluency,
  not THEIR recognition)

Good spines:
- A pattern the reader has felt but never articulated
- Something that explains multiple seemingly unrelated behaviors
  as one system
- A tension that, once named, makes the reader re-see their own
  history
```

**Downstream impact:** If spine selection improves, every section improves â€” the Opening drop hits harder, the Build connects more naturally, the Turn crystallizes more easily, and the Landing archetypes feel more relevant because they're addressing the reader's real tension.

### New Architectural Additions

**1. Turn fallback instruction (failure mode protection):**

The Turn is the most fragile section across models. A failed Turn (restating the Build, announcing the reframe) is WORSE than no Turn â€” the reader thinks "they're repeating themselves, they ran out of things to say about me."

```

portrait with a failed Turn.

The test: does your Turn add a NEW LENS, or does it say

```

**2. Archetype refinement â€” "specific in form, universal in feeling" (ESPECIALLY IMPORTANT):**

The archetypes' concrete details must be projectable. The detail that makes an archetype feel real also makes it specific to someone ELSE. The reader must be able to map the detail onto their own version.

```
Archetype details should be SPECIFIC IN FORM but UNIVERSAL
IN FEELING.

"She stopped painting" works â€” everyone has a thing they
stopped doing. The specificity is in the verb. The
universality is in the loss.

"She stopped attending her Tuesday pottery class at the
community center" fails â€” too specific to someone else's
life. The reader can't project themselves into it.

Test: could the reader replace the detail with their own
version and feel the same thing? If yes, it's the right
level of specificity.
```

**3. Subtitle as scanner orientation:**

The italic subtitle serves double duty â€” intrigue for linear readers, navigation for scanners. This should be an explicit instruction.

```
The italic subtitle orients scanning readers. It should hint
at the section's territory without spoiling the content.

Good: "what you've built and what it costs" (territory clear,
content unknown)
Good: "the pattern beneath the patterns" (intriguing AND
directional)
Bad: "your personality analysis" (too generic, no pull)
Bad: "the surprising connection between your work habits and
your relationships" (spoils the content)
```

**4. Reader state â€” arriving from conversation:**

The reader just finished a 25-message emotional conversation with Nerin. They arrive at the portrait exhausted but invested. The Opening must signal immediately: "I was there with you" â€” not "I've analyzed your data."

This makes specifics-in-first-3-sentences even more critical. The reader needs to feel continuity between chat-Nerin and portrait-Nerin. The first sentence should reference something SPECIFIC from the conversation â€” a moment, a phrase, a reaction â€” that tells the reader this is the same person who was just talking to them.
