## Scientific definition

Neuroticism measures emotional reactivity and sensitivity to threat, stress, and negative affect. It is often misunderstood as a flaw. In research terms, it is better understood as the intensity and persistence of emotional alarm systems.

## Low neuroticism in daily life

Lower neuroticism often looks like calm recovery after setbacks, lower baseline worry, and a stronger sense that difficult moments can be handled without spiraling. This range can bring steadiness under pressure.

## Mid-range neuroticism in daily life

Middle-range neuroticism usually means you feel stress clearly but not endlessly. Emotions register, information is taken seriously, and self-awareness often helps you come back to center.

## High neuroticism in daily life

Higher neuroticism tends to amplify anticipation, vigilance, shame, or overwhelm. It can also sharpen empathy and intuition because small shifts in tone, risk, and mood are harder to ignore.

## Facet breakdown

The six neuroticism facets show where sensitivity is concentrated, whether in anxiety, frustration, sadness, self-consciousness, impulsive coping, or vulnerability under pressure.
