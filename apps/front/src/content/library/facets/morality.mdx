<h2 id="what-morality-measures">What morality measures</h2>

Morality — called "Straightforwardness" in the NEO model — captures the degree to which you are direct, sincere, and transparent in social interactions. It reflects how much you say what you mean versus how much you manage impressions and navigate strategically.

<h2 id="when-morality-is-more-strategic">When morality is more strategic</h2>

People on the lower end navigate social situations with awareness and tact. They read between the lines, adapt their message to the audience, and protect their interests through careful positioning. This is not dishonesty — it is social intelligence applied to self-preservation. They are skilled at diplomacy and negotiation.

<h2 id="when-morality-is-direct">When morality is direct</h2>

People on the higher end say what they mean and mean what they say. Honesty and fairness guide their interactions. They follow the rules, avoid manipulation, and expect the same from others. This directness builds trust with people who value transparency, though it can sometimes come across as blunt in settings that prize subtlety.

<h2 id="how-it-shows-up-in-daily-life">How it shows up in daily life</h2>

Morality shows up in how you communicate difficult truths. Higher scorers deliver feedback straight, disclose conflicts of interest proactively, and feel uncomfortable with spin. Lower scorers may frame the same information more carefully, choosing their words to achieve a specific outcome. Both approaches have costs and benefits — one maximises clarity, the other maximises influence.
