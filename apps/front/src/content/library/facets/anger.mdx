## What anger measures

Anger — called "Angry Hostility" in the NEO model — captures the tendency to experience frustration, irritation, and indignation, and how quickly those feelings surface. It is not about aggression or behaviour; it is about the emotional activation itself.

## When anger is slower to rise

People on the lower end have a high threshold for frustration. It takes a lot to push them past their limit. They absorb slights, tolerate inconvenience, and remain composed in situations that would irritate others. This patience is a social asset, though it can sometimes mean legitimate grievances go unexpressed.

## When anger flares faster

People on the higher end feel irritation quickly when things seem unjust. That fire can motivate them to push back, set boundaries, and advocate for change. They experience anger as a signal — something is wrong and needs attention. The challenge is directing that energy constructively rather than letting it spill over into relationships.

## How it shows up in daily life

Anger shows up in response to perceived unfairness, incompetence, or boundary violations. Higher scorers may snap at slow service, feel disproportionate frustration over small inefficiencies, or become visibly upset during disagreements. Lower scorers may absorb the same provocations with barely a ripple. Neither response is inherently healthier — what matters is whether the anger serves a purpose and whether it is expressed in ways that solve the problem rather than create new ones.
