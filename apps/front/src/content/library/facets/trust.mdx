<h2 id="what-trust-measures">What trust measures</h2>

Trust captures the degree to which you believe that other people generally have good intentions. It is not naivety — it is a default assumption about human nature that shapes how you approach new relationships, collaborations, and social transactions.

<h2 id="when-trust-is-more-guarded">When trust is more guarded</h2>

People on the lower end verify before they trust. They watch actions over words, looking for consistency before extending confidence. This scepticism is protective — it reduces exposure to exploitation and ensures that trust, once given, is well-placed. New relationships are approached with respectful caution rather than open arms.

<h2 id="when-trust-comes-readily">When trust comes readily</h2>

People on the higher end extend trust by default, believing that most people mean well until proven otherwise. This openness accelerates relationship-building and collaboration. They are easier to work with and quicker to give others the benefit of the doubt, though they may occasionally be caught off guard by bad actors.

<h2 id="how-it-shows-up-in-daily-life">How it shows up in daily life</h2>

Trust influences how you interpret ambiguous social signals. Higher scorers assume a missed call was accidental; lower scorers wonder whether it was deliberate. Higher scorers lend things freely; lower scorers keep a mental ledger. Both orientations have adaptive value — one builds social capital fast, the other preserves it carefully.
