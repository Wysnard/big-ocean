<h2 id="what-orderliness-measures">What orderliness measures</h2>

Orderliness — called "Order" in the NEO model — captures how strongly you are drawn to structure, systems, and neatness in your physical and mental environments. It is about the felt need for things to be in their place.

<h2 id="when-orderliness-is-more-relaxed">When orderliness is more relaxed</h2>

People on the lower end thrive in loose, adaptive settings where they can shift gears quickly without rigid structure slowing them down. Their desks may look chaotic to an outsider, but they often know exactly where things are. Flexibility and spontaneity come naturally; strict schedules feel like unnecessary constraints.

<h2 id="when-orderliness-is-strong">When orderliness is strong</h2>

People on the higher end notice when things are out of place and feel compelled to fix it. They maintain clear systems — for files, calendars, routines, even kitchen drawers. This need for structure creates efficiency and reliability, though it can also make unexpected disruptions feel disproportionately stressful.

<h2 id="how-it-shows-up-in-daily-life">How it shows up in daily life</h2>

Orderliness is visible in how people manage their physical and digital spaces. Higher scorers colour-code their calendars, keep inboxes at zero, and feel genuine satisfaction when a room is tidy. Lower scorers may leave dishes in the sink while they finish something more interesting, and their creative process may look messy but still produce excellent results. The facet describes preference, not capability.
