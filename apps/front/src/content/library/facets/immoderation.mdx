<h2 id="what-immoderation-measures">What immoderation measures</h2>

Immoderation — sometimes called "Impulsiveness" in the NEO model — captures the difficulty of resisting cravings, urges, and immediate desires. It reflects the strength of impulse relative to self-regulation: how hard it is to say no to something you want right now.

<h2 id="when-immoderation-is-lower">When immoderation is lower</h2>

People on the lower end exercise steady self-control, keeping impulses in check without much internal struggle. Delayed gratification comes naturally — they can walk past the dessert tray, stick to a budget, and resist the urge to check their phone during focused work. This discipline keeps them on track but may sometimes make them rigid about routines.

<h2 id="when-immoderation-is-higher">When immoderation is higher</h2>

People on the higher end experience cravings that hit hard and fast. They act on desire in the moment, worrying about consequences later. This impulsivity is not a character flaw — it is a stronger pull from the reward system. It can bring spontaneity, intensity, and a capacity for full-bodied enjoyment, but it also creates friction with long-term goals if left unchecked.

<h2 id="how-it-shows-up-in-daily-life">How it shows up in daily life</h2>

Immoderation shapes spending habits, eating patterns, and how you respond to temptation. Higher scorers may impulse-buy, eat past fullness, stay up later than planned bingeing a show, or struggle to maintain habits that require consistent restraint. Lower scorers may stick to plans with minimal willpower expenditure. Knowing where you fall helps you design your environment — removing temptation for higher scorers, adding spontaneity for lower ones.
