<h2 id="what-gregariousness-measures">What gregariousness measures</h2>

Gregariousness captures your preference for the company of others versus solitude. It reflects how much energy you draw from social contact and how strongly you seek out shared experiences, gatherings, and group activity.

<h2 id="when-gregariousness-is-lower">When gregariousness is lower</h2>

People on the lower end recharge in solitude and prefer meaningful one-on-one conversations over crowded social events. Alone time is not loneliness — it is restoration. They may enjoy social contact in small doses but feel drained after extended periods of group interaction, and their best thinking often happens in quiet.

<h2 id="when-gregariousness-is-higher">When gregariousness is higher</h2>

People on the higher end come alive in a crowd, drawing energy from lively gatherings and seeking out shared experiences. An empty evening feels like a missed opportunity; a full calendar feels like a gift. They thrive on the buzz of social settings and often play a connecting role — introducing people, suggesting plans, and keeping groups together.

<h2 id="how-it-shows-up-in-daily-life">How it shows up in daily life</h2>

Gregariousness influences how you spend free time and recover from stress. Higher scorers may call a friend before they call it a night, plan group trips, and feel restless after a quiet weekend. Lower scorers may protect their evenings, decline social invitations without guilt, and feel most creative after a stretch of uninterrupted solitude. Healthy relationships accommodate both patterns.
