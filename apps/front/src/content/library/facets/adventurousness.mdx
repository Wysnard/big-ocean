<h2 id="what-adventurousness-measures">What adventurousness measures</h2>

Adventurousness — called "Actions" in the NEO model — captures your willingness to seek out new activities, environments, and experiences rather than staying within familiar routines. It is about behavioural novelty-seeking, distinct from intellectual curiosity or thrill-seeking.

<h2 id="when-adventurousness-is-lower">When adventurousness is lower</h2>

People on the lower end draw comfort and momentum from familiar routines and trusted methods. They prefer consistency not out of fear but because repeated practice deepens skill, and stable patterns free up mental bandwidth for the things that matter. Change is accepted when it is clearly justified, not pursued for its own sake.

<h2 id="when-adventurousness-is-higher">When adventurousness is higher</h2>

People on the higher end feel pulled toward the unfamiliar. Trying a new restaurant, taking an unexpected route, or experimenting with a different workflow gives them energy. The novelty itself is part of the reward — even when the new thing turns out to be worse than the old thing, the experience of trying is still satisfying.

<h2 id="how-it-shows-up-in-daily-life">How it shows up in daily life</h2>

Adventurousness shows up in how you structure your time and respond to opportunity. Higher scorers may rearrange their weekend plans on a whim, volunteer for unfamiliar projects at work, or travel without a fixed itinerary. Lower scorers may have a well-optimised weekly rhythm and feel genuinely satisfied by it. The contrast is visible in vacation styles, food choices, career pivots, and how quickly someone says "yes" to something they have never done before.
