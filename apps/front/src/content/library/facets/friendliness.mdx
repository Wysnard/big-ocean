<h2 id="what-friendliness-measures">What friendliness measures</h2>

Friendliness — called "Warmth" in the NEO model — captures how quickly and naturally you express positive regard toward others, especially people you do not yet know well. It is about interpersonal warmth: the ease with which you make others feel welcome and at ease.

<h2 id="when-friendliness-is-more-reserved">When friendliness is more reserved</h2>

People on the lower end warm up gradually, keeping a respectful distance until a genuine connection earns their openness. They are not cold — they simply allocate warmth selectively, investing it in relationships that have proven their worth. New acquaintances experience politeness; close friends experience deep loyalty.

<h2 id="when-friendliness-comes-easily">When friendliness comes easily</h2>

People on the higher end put others at ease quickly, radiating warmth that makes strangers feel like friends in minutes. Their openness is not calculated — it is a natural expression of interest in other people. This warmth builds rapport fast, which is an asset in social, sales, and caregiving contexts.

<h2 id="how-it-shows-up-in-daily-life">How it shows up in daily life</h2>

Friendliness shapes first impressions and social comfort. Higher scorers smile readily, ask personal questions early, and remember names. Lower scorers may take several encounters to open up, but once they do, their connections tend to be deep and durable. The facet describes social style, not social capacity — reserved people can be just as well-liked; they simply build trust on a different timeline.
