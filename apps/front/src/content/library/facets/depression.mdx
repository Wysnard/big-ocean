## What depression measures

Depression in the Big Five is not a clinical diagnosis — it captures the tendency to experience feelings of sadness, guilt, hopelessness, and loneliness as part of normal emotional variation. It reflects how deeply and how long negative moods settle in after setbacks.

## When depression is lower

People on the lower end bounce back quickly from disappointment. They keep a bright outlook even when things get tough, and negative moods tend to pass through rather than taking root. This resilience helps them maintain momentum and optimism, making them a stabilising force for those around them.

## When depression is higher

People on the higher end experience heavy feelings that linger. Sadness can settle in deeply after loss or failure, and it does not lift as quickly as they wish it would. But that same depth fuels empathy, artistic sensitivity, and a rich inner life. They feel things fully — the cost is heaviness; the gift is depth.

## How it shows up in daily life

Depression as a personality facet influences recovery speed and emotional baseline. Higher scorers may need more time after a setback, experience guilt more readily, and have periods where motivation drops without clear cause. Lower scorers may process the same setback and move on within hours. Understanding this as a trait rather than a flaw can help — it means adjusting expectations around recovery time rather than judging yourself for not bouncing back fast enough.
