<h2 id="what-sympathy-measures">What sympathy measures</h2>

Sympathy — called "Tender-Mindedness" in the NEO model — captures how strongly you are moved by the suffering and needs of others. It reflects an emotional response to other people's situations, not just an intellectual understanding of them.

<h2 id="when-sympathy-is-more-analytical">When sympathy is more analytical</h2>

People on the lower end approach others' problems with clear-eyed analysis, offering solutions rather than just comfort. They care, but their caring takes the form of practical help rather than emotional resonance. This pragmatic empathy can be especially valuable in crisis situations where clear thinking matters more than emotional validation.

<h2 id="when-sympathy-is-strong">When sympathy is strong</h2>

People on the higher end feel what others feel. Their struggles move them, and that compassion shows in how they respond — with warmth, patience, and a willingness to sit with someone else's pain rather than rushing to fix it. This emotional attunement builds deep bonds and makes them natural caregivers, counsellors, and advocates.

<h2 id="how-it-shows-up-in-daily-life">How it shows up in daily life</h2>

Sympathy shapes how you respond to a friend's bad news, a tragic story in the news, or a colleague's frustration. Higher scorers may tear up during movies, feel heavy after hearing about someone else's loss, and instinctively prioritise emotional support. Lower scorers may acknowledge the situation and move quickly to problem-solving, which can feel cold to sympathetic types but is often exactly what the situation needs.
