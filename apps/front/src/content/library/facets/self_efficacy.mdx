<h2 id="what-self-efficacy-measures">What self-efficacy measures</h2>

Self-efficacy — called "Competence" in the NEO model — captures the degree to which you trust your own ability to accomplish tasks and handle what life throws at you. It is not about actual skill level; it is about perceived capability and the confidence that flows from it.

<h2 id="when-self-efficacy-is-more-cautious">When self-efficacy is more cautious</h2>

People on the lower end tend to seek input, support, and confirmation before committing to action. They may underestimate their own readiness and prefer to have the right conditions in place before jumping in. This caution can protect against overcommitment but may also slow down decision-making when the stakes are modest.

<h2 id="when-self-efficacy-is-strong">When self-efficacy is strong</h2>

People on the higher end tackle challenges with quiet confidence. They trust that they can figure things out, even in unfamiliar territory. This self-trust makes them more likely to take initiative, volunteer for stretch assignments, and recover quickly from setbacks — because they believe the next attempt will be better.

<h2 id="how-it-shows-up-in-daily-life">How it shows up in daily life</h2>

Self-efficacy influences how you approach new responsibilities. Higher scorers tend to say "I'll handle it" before they have a full plan, trusting themselves to adapt. Lower scorers tend to ask "What do I need first?" — gathering resources, checking in with others, and building a safety net before acting. Teams benefit from both: one style generates momentum, the other prevents blind spots.
