<h2 id="what-artistic-interests-measures">What artistic interests measures</h2>

Artistic interests — originally called "Aesthetics" in the NEO model — captures the degree to which beauty, art, music, and sensory experience draw your attention and shape your emotional life. It is not about talent or training; it is about how strongly aesthetic experiences register.

<h2 id="when-aesthetic-sensitivity-is-lower">When aesthetic sensitivity is lower</h2>

People on the lower end focus on function over form. They evaluate a tool, a room, or a piece of writing by whether it works, not how it looks or sounds. This practical lens can be an advantage when decisions need to be fast and outcome-driven. Beauty is appreciated when it shows up, but it is not a primary filter for everyday choices.

<h2 id="when-aesthetic-sensitivity-is-higher">When aesthetic sensitivity is higher</h2>

People on the higher end notice beauty reflexively — a well-set table, an unusual colour palette, the rhythm of a sentence. These moments carry emotional weight and often inform decisions: choosing a workspace partly for its light, or gravitating toward products that feel carefully crafted. Art, design, and music become sources of genuine nourishment, not optional extras.

<h2 id="how-it-shows-up-in-daily-life">How it shows up in daily life</h2>

Artistic interests shapes what catches your eye and what you linger on. Higher scorers tend to curate their environments with more intention, invest time in cultural experiences, and find that beauty recharges them. Lower scorers may barely register the same stimuli, not because they are indifferent but because their attention is allocated elsewhere — toward efficiency, logic, or concrete results.
