<h2 id="what-liberalism-measures">What liberalism measures</h2>

Liberalism — called "Values" in the NEO model — captures the readiness to re-examine social, political, and cultural conventions rather than accepting them as given. It is not about any specific political position; it is about the psychological orientation toward inherited norms.

<h2 id="when-convention-is-valued">When convention is valued</h2>

People on the lower end respect established values and time-tested traditions. They see stability, continuity, and shared norms as the foundation on which trust and cooperation are built. Questioning a convention requires a strong reason — change for its own sake feels reckless rather than progressive.

<h2 id="when-re-examination-comes-naturally">When re-examination comes naturally</h2>

People on the higher end instinctively question "the way things are done." They believe that assumptions deserve periodic stress-testing and that progress often requires uncomfortable conversations about inherited beliefs. This orientation is not contrarian for its own sake — it reflects a genuine conviction that better arrangements are discoverable.

<h2 id="how-it-shows-up-in-daily-life">How it shows up in daily life</h2>

Liberalism shapes how you respond to rules, traditions, and social expectations. Higher scorers may challenge workplace norms, advocate for policy changes, or feel uncomfortable participating in rituals they have not personally endorsed. Lower scorers may value those same rituals as sources of community and meaning. Both orientations serve important functions — one preserves what works, the other clears the way for what could work better.
