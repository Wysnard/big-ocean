<h2 id="what-altruism-measures">What altruism measures</h2>

Altruism captures the strength of your active concern for the welfare of others — how readily you notice what people need and how naturally you step in to help, even when no one asks.

<h2 id="when-altruism-is-more-selective">When altruism is more selective</h2>

People on the lower end handle their own needs first and offer help selectively. Their energy goes where it counts — they are generous with people they care about but do not feel compelled to solve every problem they encounter. This boundary protects their capacity and ensures their help is meaningful when it comes.

<h2 id="when-altruism-flows-freely">When altruism flows freely</h2>

People on the higher end notice what people need and step in without being asked. Helping others is not an obligation — it is a natural reflex. They volunteer, check in on friends, and offer assistance to strangers. This generosity builds deep goodwill and rich social networks, though it can lead to burnout if giving is not balanced with receiving.

<h2 id="how-it-shows-up-in-daily-life">How it shows up in daily life</h2>

Altruism influences how you respond to requests and how much unsolicited support you provide. Higher scorers are the first to offer a ride, bring soup to a sick neighbour, or stay late to help a colleague. Lower scorers are just as capable of generosity but allocate it more deliberately — choosing their moments rather than responding to every need they see.
